Why Quantum computers need to be cold.


        Recently real strides have been made to realize the potential quantum computing. Quantum computing is computation through quantum phenomenon as an alternative to traditional transistor logic. D-Wave began to performing computation by quantum annealing at the turn of the century and their systems are now commercially available. “In quantum mechanics the properties that we’re trying to harness are very easily washed out by the movement of the atoms in the processor...  ” Geordie Rose D-Wave The Quantum Computing Company™. Meaning that the thing doesn’t work unless it’s cold. To understand what is being said here let’s look at a picture that captures the essence of quantum computing.  

        Here (left) we see the quantum bit’s ability to, at a given time, computationally represent zero and one simultaneously. This means that instead of iteratively testing each possible state for a set of bits, quantum computers are able to interrogate an entire set of possible states at one time.  It is important to realize that quantum superposition is not the same as having a classical computer, and while classical computer bits cannot compute using the principles of entanglement, we are only able to read from and control quantum computers with classical computing technology. Using computer numerical control at super low temperatures (near absolute zero) D-Wave effectively freezes a set of q bits into the coolest possible position for the a given state of coupling, this process is known as annealing. Entangling the waveforms of these multiple bits requires the the manipulation of superconducting couplers, super cooled niobium loops in the case of  D-Wave’s fabrication. Utilizing phenomenon beyond the scope of classical computers  quantum annealing provides a fast solution for finding global extrema in large search space. So what would happen if it wasn’t cold? Well first off the qbits would not hold one state, flipping up and down due to heat. Furthermore, the niobium loops wouldn’t be superconductive meaning that these loops would be liable to change energies of magnetic field within and around them and therefore affecting the result of the anneal. Annealing is not the classic form of quantum computing in fact it isn’t within the quantum information standard.  Regardless D-Wave has been able to double the number of qbits on a chip 9  times in about as many years. Considering that the advantage for an annealing machine over a traditional computer (in terms of the number of steps required to solve certain problems) is a directly proportional to the number states n bits can represent, or 2 raised to the power of the number of bits. Each time the number of bits is doubled, the advantage over traditional computers is squared.

        Let’s look at a really simple example. Say that we want to know which number between 1 and 1000 is closest to 712. Intuitively we know the answer is 712, but let’s for the sake of investigation see how a traditional computer, classic quantum computer, and annealing machine would arrive at this number. First a traditional computer with the most naive approach. Starting at one end of the range (1) we measure measure the distance from this test point to the target 712 and if that distance is less than the know least difference the computer stores 1 as being the closest also saving the difference 711, next the computer moves to the next number (2) repeating the process and replacing the closest number found yet and the respective difference each time a smaller respective difference is found. None the less the illustration of the fundamental shift in paradigm is the same, rather than a program describing an explicit iteration, a coupling of bits and a one shot execution provides an implicate coverage of all the possible states. It is important to note that IBM’s quantum computer is not a quantum annealer, so where D-Wave utilizes a set of weighted couplings between bits, IBM’s quantum experience uses the more traditional quantum state coupling, notice the X,Y,Z,H,S,T, couplers in the IBM quantum experience whereas programming D-Wave involves the setting and scaling of weights in pairwise bit matrices weight and strength. This difference in implementation is what makes IBM’s 5 bit system similarly ground breaking as D-wave’s 128 bit system.

        So Quantum computers require super cold temperatures to achieve superconductivity and entanglement. Also when the validity of a computational outcome requires encoding to a ground state hamiltonian (as in the case of an annealing machine) unwanted thermal energy may appear in the form of a flipped qbit or divergence of waveforms throughout the system relative to nominal behaviour in an `ideal Nb superconductor` i.e. a wrong answer.

    IBM’s error checking implementations and distribution of access to quantum computers positions them as a leader in quantum computing and from such a position they are pretty well listened too when they publish things like their definition of quantum computing categories. Almost poking at D-Wave Big-Blue says “The quantum annealer is least powerful and most restrictive form of quantum computers. The consensus of the scientific community is that a quantum annealer has no known advantages over conventional computing." as IBM Specifies one of 3 types of quantum computers Ironically their 5-bit system offered to the community doesn’t actually fit any of the category. Their classic quantum computer consisting of a small number qubits (accompanied by the option to run a classical computer simulation of the system) par their definitions is not the universal quantum computer, analog quantum computer, or quantum annealer. This is because of the number of bit’s and not the nature of the computing. In other words, the difference between IBM’s Quantum experience and the analog or universal quantum computer is on of scale whereas D-Wave’s system differs from the XYZTSH paradigm specified by traditional quantum information theory, in a fundamental sense.
